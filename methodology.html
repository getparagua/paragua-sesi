Methodology

High-level overview (v0.1, frozen)

SESI is designed to be simple to read: a 0–100 historical ranking of stress. This page explains what SESI measures, how to interpret it, and how the public validation is computed.

Concept

SESI measures stress regimes in the Spanish power market. It is not trying to predict a specific price. It is trying to answer: “Are we in a calm regime, or in a regime where the market tends to move a lot?”

SESI combines three complementary dimensions:

  1. Price level: how far prices are from a long-term historical baseline
  2. Short-term tension: unusual recent price behavior
  3. Market turbulence: how intense recent volatility has been

SESI is a benchmark for risk communication and regime monitoring. It is not a trading signal.

How to read a percentile

SESI is published as a number from 0 to 100. Think of it like a ranking.

If SESI is 80, it means today looks more stressed than about 80% of historical days (in the chosen reference set).

Percentiles are historical rankings, not probabilities. A percentile does not mean “80% chance of something happening”. It only means “this is high compared to history”.

Three views: Realized vs Structural vs Expected

SESI is published through three aligned views:

  • SESI (realized): what is happening now in spot (OMIE day-ahead).
  • SESI_structural: how tight the background regime is vs the pre-2022 reference period.
  • Expected stress (OMIP): what forward prices imply for the next months (rank vs pre-2022).

Comparing Expected vs Structural helps identify dislocations: situations where forward prices look much calmer (or much more stressed) than the background regime.

Dislocation is expressed in percentile points: D = Expected − Structural.

Regime map (quadrants)

SESI also supports a simple regime map using two axes:

  • X-axis: SESI_structural (0–100)
  • Y-axis: SESI (0–100)

Using a threshold (e.g. 80), each day falls into one of four regimes:

  • Calm: low realized, low structural
  • Latent risk: low realized, high structural
  • Realized stress: high realized, low structural
  • Full crisis: high realized, high structural

The threshold is a practical way to separate “normal” from “high-stress” regimes. It is not a rule and it is not a forecast.

Public validation: “what usually happens next”

SESI includes a public validation that answers a simple question:

When SESI was high in the past, how much did the market typically move afterwards?

This is computed using historical conditional outcomes. That means: we look at days matching a condition (example: SESI > 80), and then measure what happened in the next 7 / 14 / 30 days.

Validation is descriptive, not predictive. It summarizes history. It does not claim an edge or a trading rule.

Outcome metrics (simple definitions)

  • Future max absolute move (7D / 14D / 30D): in the next N days, what was the largest change from today’s price, regardless of direction.
  • Future max up move: the largest increase from today’s price within the next N days.
  • Future max down move: the largest decrease from today’s price within the next N days.
  • Volatility expansion (7D): how much 7-day volatility increased after today compared to the 7 days before today.
  • Spike frequency (next 30D): the share of days in the next 30 days where the daily move exceeded a fixed threshold (e.g., 20 €/MWh) or a volatility-scaled threshold.

In the dashboard, we usually show medians (typical outcomes) and sample sizes (how many historical days were included).

Data sources

  • OMIE — Spanish day-ahead marginal prices
  • OMIP — Iberian power futures settlement prices (Month contracts)
  • Yahoo Finance — continuous TTF natural gas futures (TTF=F)

No proprietary, internal, or confidential data sources are used.
